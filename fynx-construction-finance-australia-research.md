## Executive Summary

The Australian construction finance market is large, fragmented, and structurally misaligned with modern methods of construction.

For consumers, mainstream construction loans are still designed around traditional on-site delivery:

- fixed-price building contracts
- licensed builders
- progress draws at slab, frame, lock-up, fit-out, and completion
- interest-only repayments during construction
- valuation and inspection checkpoints

That works reasonably well for standard detached housing, but it breaks down when a large share of value is created off-site in a factory before the home is affixed to land.

For developers, the market splits into two layers:

- banks and ADIs with conservative underwriting, tighter leverage, and historically strong presale expectations
- non-bank and private credit lenders offering more flexibility, speed, and structured capital, but generally at higher cost and without any real product focus on modular/prefab delivery

The best evidence that a real financing gap exists is that CommBank spent 2025 creating a dedicated prefab policy, joining prefabAUS, and sponsoring work on a standard-form contract for modern methods of construction. That is a strong signal that the market has not been built for off-site delivery.

At the same time, Australia has a housing supply problem severe enough that governments, lenders, and housing agencies are actively searching for delivery models that are faster, more repeatable, and less dependent on scarce site labour. The National Housing Supply and Affordability Council has said supply remains well below demand and that structural constraints include low productivity and low innovation in construction. Housing Australia has highlighted modular construction as an innovation path, and CommBank has already moved to make prefab finance easier.

This creates a credible entry point for FYNX.

The core opportunity is not "be another lender." The opportunity is to become the specialist finance layer for modern construction by combining:

- modular-aware underwriting
- digital origination and approvals
- manufacturer accreditation
- milestone-based off-site funding
- project monitoring data
- a customer experience built around certainty and speed

If done well, FYNX can sit between traditional home lending, specialist non-bank finance, and construction-tech enablement.

The sharpest early wedge is:

- owner-occupiers and investors building prefab/modular homes on owned or newly purchased land
- small to mid-sized developers delivering modular townhouse, infill, social/affordable, and regional housing projects

The strategic principle should be:

`FYNX finances modern construction; Fabulr helps deliver it; the two work together but are not the same company.`

That separation matters for brand trust, governance, credit risk, and long-term scalability.

## 1. The Australian Market Context

### 1.1 Australia has a real housing supply deficit

The housing backdrop is not just "difficult"; it is structurally undersupplied.

The National Housing Supply and Affordability Council (NHSAC) reported in the **State of the Housing System 2025** that:

- housing affordability deteriorated in 2024
- only **177,000 dwellings** were completed in 2024
- underlying demand was about **223,000 dwellings**
- **938,000 dwellings** are forecast to be completed during the Housing Accord period, below the **1.2 million** target
- net new supply is expected to be **79,000 dwellings below expected new demand** over the Accord period

NHSAC also said structural constraints remain the main barriers to supply, including:

- labour shortages
- low construction productivity
- low innovation
- fragmented planning/regulatory settings
- financing costs and project feasibility pressure

Implication for FYNX:

- there is national policy pressure to support faster and more productive housing delivery
- any finance product that can unlock modular/prefab supply is speaking to a real macro problem, not a niche trend

### 1.2 Approvals remain weak in the most recent accessible ABS release

In the ABS **Building Approvals, Australia** release for **January 2026**:

- total dwellings approved fell **7.2%** to **14,564**
- private sector dwellings excluding houses fell **24.5%** to **4,393**
- private sector houses rose **1.1%** to **9,753**
- total residential building value fell **1.2%** to **$9.48 billion**

Implication for FYNX:

- the higher-density pipeline remains soft
- feasibility and capital access are still constraining new supply
- any product that helps smaller modular developments move from approval to funding has market relevance

### 1.3 Construction productivity is weak and traditional delivery is under strain

The 2025 CEDA paper hosted through the RBA conference materials, **Size matters: Why construction productivity is so weak**, argues that construction has become dominated by smaller firms, that smaller firms are less productive, and that tax/settings/regulation contribute to firms staying small.

NHSAC also highlighted that modern methods of construction can reduce build times, improve labour efficiency, and reduce waste, but market uptake remains limited.

Implication for FYNX:

- financing should not assume the current dominant delivery model is the right model
- FYNX can position itself as the finance platform for a more repeatable, data-rich, productivity-led construction system

### 1.4 Builder and contractor insolvency risk is a major underwriting issue

ASIC’s insolvency reporting shows construction remains the largest failure cohort.

Examples from ASIC sources:

- in annual 2023-24 external administrations, construction represented **27%** of company failures
- in an earlier ASIC release, registered liquidators reported construction as the industry with the highest share of insolvency reports

Implication for FYNX:

- builder/manufacturer failure risk is one of the biggest reasons lenders stay conservative
- a FYNX product must be designed around insolvency resilience, not just approvals speed

## 2. How Consumers Obtain Construction Finance Today

### 2.1 The mainstream product structure is conventional and fairly standardised

Across major lenders and mutuals, the basic consumer construction loan model looks similar:

- the borrower has land or buys land
- the borrower signs a building contract
- the loan funds construction in stages
- the borrower pays interest only on funds drawn during construction
- lender valuations/inspections happen at key stages
- the loan converts to standard principal-and-interest repayments once construction is complete

Examples from lender materials:

- ANZ explains construction loans through progress payments tied to a fixed-price building contract
- Westpac describes drawdowns through slab, frame, lock-up, fit-out and completion stages
- Bankwest notes valuations before construction, at slab/base, and before final draw
- CommBank’s construction loan guide requires construction to commence within 12 months of the loan disclosure date
- Macquarie notes that changes to builder, contract, or scope may trigger new valuations and reassessment

### 2.2 Typical consumer construction finance requirements

Common requirements include:

- fixed-price building contract
- licensed or registered builder
- plans and permits
- builder licence details
- builder insurance or relevant warranty/insurance documents
- progress payment invoices
- satisfactory valuation evidence
- commencement within a set timeframe, often around 12 months

Some lenders are more flexible than others, but the system is built for traditional staged on-site construction.

### 2.3 Main consumer pain points in standard construction finance

Even before prefab enters the picture, borrowers face friction:

- long approval and document cycles
- little clarity on variations and overruns
- staged valuations that can delay payments
- exposure to builder delays and insolvency
- cash flow pressure during construction
- manual and stressful coordination between borrower, broker, lender, builder, and valuer

### 2.4 Why prefab/modular is harder for consumer finance

This is the most important part of the market gap for FYNX.

Traditional construction finance assumes:

- value is created progressively on the land
- the lender’s security improves as the house is built on-site
- progress stages align with visible site milestones

Prefab/modular breaks that assumption because:

- substantial value is created off-site in a factory
- the lender may need to fund before the home is affixed to the land
- the land may be the only practical security until delivery and affixation
- contract forms are less standardised
- insurer/warranty settings vary by state
- transport, craning, installation, and site services create hybrid milestones that do not fit standard bank workflows

CommBank’s prefab pages are especially useful because they make this problem explicit:

- CommBank says it is the **first major bank** to offer finance during off-site construction
- it allows progress funding while the home is being built off-site
- for accredited/assessed manufacturers, customers can access up to **80%** of the build contract price during off-site construction
- for non-assessed manufacturers, funding is lower
- CommBank also explicitly warns that before the home is affixed, the **land may be the sole security**, creating negative equity risk if the builder/manufacturer fails or does not complete

This is the core market failure:

- mainstream construction lending is land-and-site based
- modular value creation is factory-and-logistics based

### 2.5 The significance of CommBank’s prefab move

CommBank’s 2025 prefab initiative is one of the clearest signals in the market.

CommBank said in January 2025 that:

- it was joining prefabAUS as the first bank member
- it would sponsor development of a standard-form contract for the MMC sector
- traditional contracts and finance processes do not currently map cleanly to off-site construction
- customers historically had to cover much of the upfront prefab cost themselves
- new policy settings would let customers access progress payments before affixation, up to 60% of contract price generally and up to 80% with accredited CommBank prefab manufacturers

That tells us:

1. The financing gap is real.
2. Standard contract infrastructure is still immature.
3. Banks believe prefab matters, but they are solving it cautiously and through accredited panels.

Implication for FYNX:

- the market is now validated, but still under-served
- FYNX cannot win by being "the first"
- FYNX can still win by being broader, faster, digitally-native, and modular-specific from day one

## 3. How Property Developers Obtain Construction Finance Today

### 3.1 The developer market is two-tiered

Developer construction finance in Australia broadly splits into:

#### Tier A: Banks and ADIs

Strengths:

- lower cost of capital
- stronger customer recognition
- ability to support larger and repeat borrowers

Constraints:

- slower approvals
- tighter leverage
- stricter policy and credit criteria
- less appetite for first-time or lightly experienced developers
- less comfort with land-banking, pre-development risk, or unusual delivery models

#### Tier B: Non-bank lenders, private credit, and specialist originators

Strengths:

- more flexible structures
- higher leverage in some cases
- faster approvals
- ability to fund more complex or time-sensitive situations
- willingness to provide mezzanine, preferred equity, residual stock, or bridging solutions

Constraints:

- higher price
- more fragmented market
- broker/intermediary dependence
- less standardised customer experience
- usually property-risk focused rather than delivery-system focused

### 3.2 Prudential settings and bank conservatism still shape the market

APRA remains clear that commercial property and development lending can be a major source of loss when underwriting weakens.

In APRA’s 2018 commercial real estate update:

- APRA described poorly underwritten CRE lending as a historic source of bank loss and even bank failure
- APRA said banks had tightened underwriting since its thematic review
- APRA observed maximum LVRs around **65%**
- APRA observed banks had increasingly moved to **100% qualifying presales to debt cover** for residential property development
- lower LVRs were typical for land-bank and pre-development lending

In February 2025, APRA clarified that its old reference to 100% presales was a reflection of market practice at the time, **not a formal prudential minimum**. That is important.

What it means:

- the regulator is not mandating a universal 100% presales rule
- but banks still remain cautious because commercial property/development risk is cyclical and capital-intensive
- there is room for better data-led underwriting models, but bank behaviour will likely remain conservative

### 3.3 Common developer underwriting expectations

Across market sources, common requirements include:

- equity contribution, often meaningful
- project feasibility modelling
- development approvals
- consultant reports
- construction contracts or builder quotes
- presales, where relevant
- clear exit strategy
- contingency planning around costs and timing

Market guidance pages from non-bank lenders and advisers commonly reference:

- equity contribution in the range of roughly **10-25%** for some non-bank deals
- leverage measured against LVR or total development cost
- mezzanine or preferred equity to fill gaps in the capital stack
- land acquisition, construction, and residual stock finance as distinct product categories

### 3.4 Current developer pain points

The main pain points are:

- weak feasibility due to build costs and finance costs
- difficulty meeting presale hurdles
- limited appetite for smaller or unusual projects
- capital stack complexity
- short tenors on land and pre-development facilities
- refinancing risk at completion or residual stock stage
- slow approvals when time-to-site matters
- poor fit between standard underwriting and modern/off-site delivery models

Wingate, Qualitas, and Metrics all signal versions of the same story:

- major banks have pulled back from parts of construction lending
- private credit has grown into the gap
- construction cost escalation and margin pressure remain underwriting concerns
- institutional capital is increasingly available for construction and real estate debt

### 3.5 The developer market is active, but not truly specialised in modular delivery

There is no shortage of capital providers in Australian development finance.

Examples include:

- Qualitas
- Wingate
- Metrics
- specialist non-bank development lenders
- mezzanine and preferred equity providers
- broker-led originators and placement firms

But most of this market is still organised around:

- property as collateral
- project feasibility and sponsor strength
- presales and exit strategy
- traditional construction monitoring

Very little of the market is built around the specific reality of modular development:

- factory production risk
- manufacturer concentration risk
- logistics and craning milestones
- separate siteworks versus module production timelines
- data-driven progress verification
- standardised off-site contract structures

That gap matters.

## 4. Why Modular and Prefab Matter Strategically

### 4.1 The time advantage is meaningful

CommBank markets prefab as potentially ready in **12 weeks** rather than a typical **12 months** for a traditional build. prefabAUS/CBA messaging also referenced roughly **10-12 weeks** in factory environments compared with **18+ months** through conventional delivery in some use cases.

NHSAC cited overseas evidence that modular construction can cut build times by **20-50%**.

### 4.2 The productivity and labour argument is strong

NHSAC and Housing Australia both point to modular/MMC as a response to:

- labour shortages
- weather disruption
- waste
- fragmented site delivery
- slow supply response

Housing Australia’s NHIF review noted:

- only around **5%** of new homes in Australia are currently prefabricated or modular
- modular construction is a genuine innovation lever
- NHIF-backed projects now include very large modular housing developments

That combination matters:

- low penetration
- strong policy interest
- real supply pressure

This is usually a good market setup for a focused new entrant.

### 4.3 Finance is one of the biggest bottlenecks to modular scale

CommBank effectively said the same thing in 2025:

- traditional contracts and bank processes are designed for on-site construction
- prefab customers historically had to self-fund large upfront amounts
- financing barriers are holding back scale

This is not just a customer acquisition problem. It is a market design problem.

## 6. The Real Gaps in the Market

This is the critical section for FYNX.

## 6.1 Gap: finance is still designed around on-site milestones, not off-site manufacturing

Traditional progress payments map poorly to:

- deposit to manufacturer
- production commencement
- module completion in factory
- transport readiness
- delivery to site
- craning/installation
- service connection and affixation

This is the biggest structural whitespace.

## 6.2 Gap: there is no dominant national brand for modular construction finance

Consumers can find:

- standard construction lenders
- a few modular-friendly brokers
- some manufacturer referrals

But there is no widely recognised Australian specialist brand that says:

`We finance modular and prefab homes nationally, and we understand the delivery model better than a generic bank or broker.`

FYNX can own this category if execution is strong.

## 6.3 Gap: manufacturer accreditation is emerging, but not yet scaled

CommBank’s assessed-manufacturer model is a clear proof point that the market needs:

- accredited factories
- standard documentation
- known delivery processes
- confidence in manufacturer financial health

But the current model is still narrow.

A new entrant can go further with:

- a broader accredited manufacturer network
- deeper monitoring and risk-scoring
- common digital contract and milestone templates
- state-by-state compliance and insurance workflow support

## 6.4 Gap: borrowers still face too much manual coordination

Construction finance is still highly manual across:

- lender
- broker
- builder/manufacturer
- valuer
- solicitor
- certifier
- borrower

A technology-led platform can reduce real friction by unifying:

- document collection
- progress claim workflow
- inspections and evidence
- loan condition tracking
- borrower updates
- exception handling

This matters both for customer satisfaction and for risk control.

## 6.5 Gap: developer finance exists, but modular specialist developer finance barely exists

There are many developer lenders. There are very few modular-native developer finance platforms.

That leaves space for products tailored to:

- townhouse and infill modular projects
- regional modular housing
- social/affordable modular projects
- projects where factory certainty can reduce site risk
- smaller developers who need more than a generic broker, but are too small for institutional attention

## 6.6 Gap: related project data is not being used as an underwriting asset

If FYNX partners closely with delivery ecosystems, it can potentially access structured data others do not have, such as:

- standard design specs
- manufacturing timelines
- variation history
- factory QA checkpoints
- transport and installation status
- supplier/payment performance
- builder/manufacturer completion history

This could support smarter underwriting than generic construction lenders can achieve.

## 6.7 Gap: the market lacks a trusted bridge between "home loan world" and "construction-tech world"

Today the customer journey is often fragmented:

- the borrower learns about modular from a builder/designer
- then tries to find finance
- then discovers the finance product does not fit the build method

FYNX can close that gap by becoming the finance layer that speaks both languages:

- consumer mortgage language
- modular construction language

## 6.8 Gap: contract infrastructure is immature

CommBank’s support for a standard MMC contract is telling. It suggests the ecosystem still lacks:

- standard form agreements banks can quickly assess
- predictable risk allocation
- consistent treatment of deposits, stage claims, title, transport, defects, and affixation

FYNX can help standardise the market rather than just lending into it.

## 11. Risk and Governance Design Principles

FYNX will only be credible if its risk architecture is stronger than its marketing.

## 11.1 Credit governance

FYNX should have:

- independent credit committee
- related-party transaction policy
- separate approvals from Fabulr commercial teams
- documented manufacturer accreditation standards
- exception policy and authority matrix

## 11.2 Security and legal structuring

Because modular creates value off-site, legal/security design matters.

Areas to design carefully with counsel:

- mortgage over land
- rights over manufactured modules prior to affixation
- assignment/step-in rights under manufacturing contracts
- treatment of deposits and milestone payments
- insolvency protections if builder/manufacturer fails mid-stream
- insurance and warranty coverage by state

## 11.3 Portfolio risk controls

Key controls should include:

- manufacturer concentration limits
- geography limits
- product-type limits
- borrower segment limits
- maximum off-site funding exposure by partner tier
- contingency reserve requirements
- revaluation triggers

## 11.4 Consumer credit compliance

If FYNX engages in consumer credit activity in Australia, ASIC guidance indicates it generally needs to:

- hold an Australian credit licence or operate under authorised representative/credit representative arrangements
- comply with consumer credit obligations where the National Credit Code applies
- maintain AFCA membership if required as a licensee

This should be treated as foundational launch work, not an afterthought.

## 15. Strategic Recommendations

### Recommendation 1

Launch FYNX as a specialist modern-construction finance brand, not a generalist lender.

### Recommendation 2

Use prefab/modular home finance as the consumer wedge, but design the brand and credit system so it can naturally expand into developer finance.

### Recommendation 3

Keep FYNX and Fabulr clearly separate entities with arm's-length governance, even if Fabulr is the anchor delivery partner at launch.

### Recommendation 4

Invest early in:

- manufacturer accreditation
- standard contract packs
- state-aware insurance/warranty workflows
- digital milestone verification

These are not back-office details; they are the product.

### Recommendation 5

Start with a funding partner / warehouse / origination model rather than trying to launch as a fully self-funded lender.

### Recommendation 6

Build a dual audience strategy:

- consumers who want certainty around prefab/modular finance
- developers who need specialist capital for modular residential projects

### Recommendation 7

Make education a major part of customer acquisition. This category is still young enough that explaining the problem is part of owning the market.
